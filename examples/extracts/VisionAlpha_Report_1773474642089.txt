Overall, these were very strong results, with upside driven squarely by Al and
with management providing more long-term visibility. The company guided to
over $100bn of Al chip revenue in FY27 (including both XPUs and Al networking),
and we see material upside to that number based on the underlying gigawatt math.
Management now sees just shy of 10GW of compute capacity in FY27. Using a rough
~520bn per GW framework gets to very large revenue numbers, though it was clear
that content per GW varies meaningfully by customer. We would view that math as
closer to a bull case, but it underscores the magnitude of the opportunity. We are
currently modeling ~$120bn of Al revenue in FY27 and see potential for upward
revisions as ASIC programs scale and Al networking continues to exceed
expectations.

Gross Margins: There had been an overhang around how to model rack margins,

Customer-owned Tooling (CoT): We do not think this debate is settled, but
management commentary was constructive. While it does not expect 100% share at
any customer indefinitely, Broadcom does not expect to see meaningful CoT share
loss for many years. It emphasized the technical challenges hyperscalers face in
designing competitive silicon and highlighted AVGO's strength in silicon design, high-
speed SerDes, advanced packaging, and large-scale cluster networking. It is also now
seeing customers move from one ASIC per year toward multiple product Lines as
workloads diversify. Engagements remain multi-year and strategic, and we do not
see significant design-out risk.

On Al more broadly, both ASIC and networking are performing well, but
networking has exceeded our expectations and is set to become a larger portion
of Al revenue next quarter (~40% of Al revenue in F2Q v5. ~33% in F1Q).
Broadcom's high-capacity switching portfolio and SerDes are clearly differentiating.
As clusters scale and scale-up bandwidth requirements increase, connectivity
becomes more central, and Broadcom is well positioned. On CPO, while Broadcom
pioneered the technology and is shipping into Limited deployments, management
does not expect high-volume adoption in the near term. Only a couple of
hyperscalers are using it at meaningful (but still modest) volumes. Reliability
concerns and limited current TCO benefit mean this is likely more of a 2028 story. Im cra)
scale-up, customers are expected to remain on direct-attach copper longer than
many feared - moving to 200G and potentially 400G SerDes before optical
becomes necessary. On optics generally, it remains a seller's market and should
remain a tailwind for the foreseeable future.
Non-Al semiconductors and software remain stable but unexciting. We are not
seeing signs of a sharp cyclical recovery, but the non-Al semis business appears
stable. Infrastructure software continues to deliver steady performance, and
management highlighted how VMW is complementary to Al workloads rather than
replaceable by them.
